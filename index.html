As winter's icy grip holds much of the nation in its embrace, a vibrant shift is underway. Fields are green, promising a bountiful harvest, and the sun begins its northward journey, signaling a time of celebration. Across the country, festivities mark this auspicious period, a moment of joy and gratitude.

Sankranti, marking the sun's transit into a new zodiac sign, holds profound religious, cultural, and natural significance across Bharath. While there are twelve Sankrantis throughout the year, Makar Sankranti, marking the sun's entry into Capricorn (Makar), is particularly important. It signifies the commencement of Uttarayan, the sun's northward journey, a period considered highly auspicious. This celestial movement symbolizes a shift from darkness to light, ignorance to wisdom, and stagnation to progress.

The Sun's northward journey.

The religious importance of Sankranti stems from its association with the deities. Uttarayan is believed to be the daytime for the gods, a time when they are more receptive to prayers and offerings. This belief underscores the importance of rituals performed during this period, such as bathing in holy rivers, offering prayers, and performing charitable acts (daana). These acts of devotion are believed to purify the soul and bring blessings.

Culturally, Sankranti is celebrated with vibrant festivities that vary regionally. These celebrations often involve community meals, kite flying, and the preparation of special foods made with sesame and jaggery, symbolizing warmth and prosperity. The act of giving (daana) is central to the celebrations, with people distributing food, clothes, and other necessities to the less fortunate. This act of charity is believed to bring good fortune and strengthen community bonds. The symbolism of bathing, especially in holy rivers like the Ganges, represents purification and the washing away of sins.

Spritual Significance

From a spiritual perspective, Sankranti represents the journey of the soul towards enlightenment. The sun's northward movement symbolizes the awakening of inner consciousness and the pursuit of spiritual growth. It is a time for introspection, contemplation, and seeking inner light. The transition from Dakshinayan (the sun's southward journey), associated with darkness and negativity, to Uttarayan is seen as a metaphor for overcoming ignorance and embracing wisdom. It encourages individuals to move towards self-realization and connect with their higher selves. The emphasis on charity and selfless service during this period reinforces the importance of detaching from material possessions and focusing on spiritual development.

Connection to Nature

Sankranti is intrinsically linked to the natural world and the harvest cycle. It marks the end of the winter solstice and the beginning of longer days, signaling the arrival of spring and the promise of a new agricultural season. In many parts of Bharath, Sankranti coincides with the harvest of winter crops, particularly those like sugarcane and sesame. The celebrations often incorporate these newly harvested crops, expressing gratitude for nature's bounty. The use of sesame and jaggery in traditional Sankranti foods is not only symbolic but also practical, as these ingredients provide warmth and energy during the winter months. The connection to nature is further emphasized by the reverence for the sun, the source of all life and energy. Sankranti serves as a reminder of the interconnectedness between humans and nature, highlighting the importance of respecting and living in harmony with the natural world.

Makar Sankranti (Many Regions)

Makar Sankranti Celebrations.

While the essence of Makar Sankranti remains consistent, the specific customs and traditions vary significantly from region to region. This diversity adds richness to the celebration. Some of the notable regional variations include:

  • Telangana & Andhra Pradesh: Bhogi Pallu – A unique tradition where children are showered with a mixture of berries, sugarcane pieces, coins, and flower petals. This ritual is believed to protect them from evil spirits and bring good health. Kite flying is also prevalent, especially in urban areas like Hyderabad.
  • Odisha: Makar Chaula – A special offering made to the deities consisting of uncooked rice, coconut, banana, jaggery, and spices. This offering is prepared in every household and shared with family and friends. The Jagannath Temple in Puri also observes special rituals.
  • Bihar: Dahi-Chura – A traditional breakfast of flattened rice (chura) mixed with yogurt (dahi), jaggery, and sesame is a staple. Kite flying is also a significant part of the festivities.
  • Goa: While not as widely celebrated as in other states, some communities observe Haldi Kumkum gatherings, where women exchange turmeric, vermilion, and gifts. The focus is more on family gatherings and feasting.
  • Karnataka: Ellu Bella – A mixture of sesame seeds (ellu), jaggery (bella), peanuts, dried coconut, and fried gram is exchanged among family and friends. This exchange symbolizes goodwill. Sugarcane is also prominent.
  • Maharashtra: Tilgul – Sesame and jaggery sweets (Tilgul) are exchanged with the saying "Tilgul ghya, god god bola" (Take Tilgul and speak sweet words). Women also participate in Haldi Kumkum ceremonies.
  • Chhattisgarh: Cherchera – A post-harvest festival around Makar Sankranti where children collect rice and grains, symbolizing sharing.
  • Rajasthan: Makar Sankranti is celebrated with fervor, especially in Jaipur and Hadoti. Til ke laddu and ghevar are special delicacies. Kite flying is a major attraction.
  • Delhi: Influenced by neighboring states, Delhi's celebration features kite flying as a dominant activity.
  • Ladakh: Due to the harsh winter, outdoor festivities are limited. Families gather for indoor celebrations and traditional meals.
  • Arunachal Pradesh: The diverse tribes have their own harvest festivals, some coinciding with Makar Sankranti, involving dances, music, and rituals.
  • Nagaland: Like Arunachal Pradesh, Nagaland's various tribes have their own post-harvest festivals sharing the theme of gratitude for the harvest.
  • Andaman & Nicobar Islands: The indigenous tribes have their own traditions, and while Makar Sankranti isn't a major festival, the theme of harvest and nature might be reflected in their customs.

Lohri (Punjab)

Lohri Bonfire.

The central ritual of Lohri is the lighting of a bonfire. People gather around the bonfire, throw sesame seeds, jaggery, sugarcane, and rewri (a type of sweet) into the flames, and sing traditional Lohri songs. This offering to the fire god, Agni, is believed to bring good fortune and ward off the cold winter. The bonfire symbolizes the passing of winter and the welcoming of warmth and new beginnings.

  • Bonfire and Offerings: As mentioned, the bonfire is the central ritual. The offerings of sesame seeds, jaggery, sugarcane, and rewri symbolize abundance and are believed to purify the surroundings.
  • Lohri Songs: Traditional Lohri songs, often sung in Punjabi, celebrate the harvest, the legend of Dulla Bhatti (a Robin Hood-like figure who rescued girls from slavery), and other folk tales.
  • Dance and Music: People dance around the bonfire to the beat of drums (dhol) and other musical instruments, creating a lively and energetic atmosphere. Bhangra, a traditional Punjabi folk dance, is often performed during Lohri celebrations.
  • Parikrama: People circumambulate the bonfire, offering prayers and seeking blessings for prosperity and good health.

Pongal (Tamil Nadu)

Pongal Preparation.

Pongal, a harvest festival celebrated with great enthusiasm in Tamil Nadu, is a four-day celebration of thanksgiving to nature, the sun god (Surya), and the cattle for their contributions to a bountiful harvest. It typically falls in mid-January, coinciding with the sun's transit into Capricorn (Makar Sankranti). Each of the four days has its own unique significance and set of rituals.

The festival's name, "Pongal," literally means "to boil over," referring to the traditional dish of sweet rice cooked in a new earthen pot until it overflows, symbolizing abundance and prosperity. The overflowing of the Pongal dish is a key moment of the festival, greeted with cheers and joyous exclamations.

  • Bhogi:The first day of Pongal, is dedicated to Lord Indra, the god of rain and clouds. It is a day of discarding old and unwanted items, symbolizing the end of the old year and the beginning of a new one. People clean their homes thoroughly and gather old belongings to burn in a bonfire called "Bhogi Mantalu." This ritual signifies the destruction of negativity and the welcoming of positive energy. New clothes are often worn, and homes are decorated.
  • Thai Pongal:The main day of the festival, is dedicated to the sun god, Surya. It is a day of thanksgiving for a bountiful harvest. The traditional dish, "Pongal," is prepared in a new earthen pot outdoors, using freshly harvested rice, milk, jaggery, and other ingredients. The pot is decorated with turmeric leaves and ginger. The moment the Pongal boils over, signifying abundance, is celebrated with joyous shouts of "Pongalo Pongal!" Offerings are made to the sun god, and the prepared Pongal is shared with family and friends. Kolams (intricate rice flour designs) are drawn in front of homes.
  • Mattu Pongal:The third day, is dedicated to cattle, which are considered essential for agriculture and rural life. Cows and bulls are bathed, decorated with garlands, bells, and painted horns, and offered special prayers. The "Jallikattu," a traditional bull-taming sport, is held in some villages, showcasing the strength and agility of the bulls and the courage of the participants. This event is a controversial aspect of Pongal, with concerns raised about animal welfare. However, it remains a significant cultural tradition in some regions.
  • Kanum PongalThe fourth and final day, is a day of visiting relatives and friends. Families gather and enjoy a picnic or outing. It is a time for strengthening social bonds and expressing gratitude to loved ones. Special prayers are offered for continued prosperity in the new year. Women perform rituals for the well-being of their brothers.

Makar Vilakku (Kerala)

Pathinettampadi are the 18 sacred steps leading to the main sanctum of the Sabarimala temple. They are highly significant in the pilgrimage and can only be climbed by those carrying the Irumudikettu (the sacred bundle)

Makaravilakku is a significant festival celebrated in Kerala, closely associated with Makar Sankranti. It marks the culmination of the annual pilgrimage to the Sabarimala Ayyappan Temple, one of the most revered Hindu pilgrimage sites in Bharath. This festival is deeply intertwined with the worship of Lord Ayyappa, a deity believed to be the son of Lord Shiva and Mohini (the female avatar of Vishnu).

The Sabarimala Temple, nestled in the Western Ghats, is dedicated to Lord Ayyappa. The temple remains open to pilgrims only during specific periods, the most important being the Mandala Kalam, a 41-day period leading up to Makaravilakku. This pilgrimage is considered highly sacred, and devotees observe strict austerities during this time.

The Makara Jyothi

The highlight of Makaravilakku is the appearance of the Makara Jyothi, a celestial light that appears on the horizon above the Ponnambalamedu hills, opposite the Sabarimala temple.It is seen as a divine manifestation of Lord Ayyappa, a symbol of his presence and blessings. The sight of the Jyothi evokes deep devotion and spiritual ecstasy among the pilgrims.The Makara Jyothi serves as a unifying symbol of faith and devotion for millions of Ayyappa devotees.

The Pilgrimage to Sabarimala

Malikappuram gets its title from the name given to female pilgrims—in remembrance to Malikappurathamma, a goddess who has a temple dedicated to her near Sabarimala.

The pilgrimage to Sabarimala, known as the Mandala Kalam, is a deeply spiritual experience. Pilgrims, known as Ayyappans, observe strict vows for 41 days before undertaking the journey. These vows include celibacy, vegetarianism, abstaining from intoxicants, and wearing black or saffron attire. They also refrain from shaving and cutting their hair. The pilgrims carry Irumudikettu, a sacred bundle containing offerings to Lord Ayyappa, including a coconut filled with ghee, rice, and other items.The journey to Sabarimala is typically undertaken on foot, traversing through dense forests and hilly terrain within the Western Ghats mountain range. The traditional trekking routes can range from roughly 45 to 60 kilometers (approximately 28 to 37 miles), depending on the chosen path. Pilgrims chant "Swamiye Saranam Ayyappa" (Lord Ayyappa, I seek refuge in you).

Tusu (Jharkhand)

Tusu Festival.

Tusu is a harvest festival celebrated in Jharkhand, West Bengal, Odisha, and Assam, deeply significant for women and young girls. While its timing often aligns with the post-harvest period and the winter solstice, it's not directly linked to Makar Sankranti. Tusu celebrates the harvest, nature, and female spirit, typically falling on the last day of the Bengali month of Poush (mid-January).

The Tusu Idol

The festival centers around the Tusu idol, often a colorful bamboo structure or decorated earthen pot, embodying nature, fertility, and prosperity. Rituals include creating and decorating the idol, singing traditional Tusu songs, and performing dances, primarily by women and girls. These celebrations express joy and gratitude for the harvest. The festival culminates in the idol's immersion in water, symbolizing the season's end and the deity's return to nature. The Tusu idol, made of bamboo or earthen pot, is adorned with colorful paper, cloth, flowers, and traditional motifs depicting rural life and mythology. This creation is a community effort, showcasing women's and girls' creativity.

Celebrations and Fairs

Fairs sell local crafts and food, creating a festive atmosphere. Local variations in customs add to the festival's rich cultural diversity. The immersion ceremony draws large crowds, marking a poignant farewell to the Tusu idol.

Poush Sankranti (West Bengal, Tripura)

Poush Sankranti, observed primarily in West Bengal and Tripura, is intrinsically linked to Makar Sankranti, marking the sun's transit into the zodiac sign of Capricorn. Celebrated on the last day of the Bengali month of Poush, it signifies the end of winter and the beginning of longer days. The festival is characterized by the preparation and consumption of an array of traditional sweets and delicacies, with "Pitha" (in West Bengal) or "Pithe" (in Tripura) taking center stage.

Pitha/Pithe: The Heart of Poush Sankranti

The focus on food, particularly Pitha/Pithe, is central to Poush Sankranti. These rice-based cakes come in numerous varieties, each with its unique flavor and preparation method. Some popular examples include:

  • Patishapta: Thin crepes made from rice flour batter, filled with a sweet coconut and jaggery mixture.
  • Puli Pithe: Dumplings made from rice flour, often filled with coconut, jaggery, or lentil paste, and cooked in milk or syrup.
  • Bhapa Pithe: Steamed rice cakes, sometimes with a sweet or savory filling.

Celebrations and Traditions

While the core focus on Pitha/Pithe remains consistent, there are some regional differences between West Bengal and Tripura. In Tripura, Awang Bangwi (a type of rice cake steamed in leaves) is a significant part of the celebration. The tribal communities of Tripura also incorporate their unique cultural practices into the festival. In West Bengal, the emphasis might be more on the variety and elaborate preparation of different types of Pitha. However, the underlying spirit of sharing, togetherness, and celebrating the harvest remains the same in both regions.

Uttarayan (Gujarat)

Uttarayan Kite Festival.

Uttarayan in Gujarat is essentially the state's vibrant celebration of Makar Sankranti, marking the sun's northward journey (Uttarayan) and the transition from winter to longer days. Celebrated on January 14th, it's synonymous with kite flying, transforming the skies into a colorful spectacle.

Kite Flying: The Highlight of Uttarayan

Kite flying is more than just a pastime during Uttarayan; it's a competitive sport. Different types of kites are used, including the popular "Patang," known for its maneuverability. Special threads called "manjha," coated with powdered glass, are used for kite fighting, aiming to cut down opponents' kites. The skies become a battleground of colorful kites, with shouts of victory erupting as a kite is cut down. The atmosphere is a mix of intense competition and lighthearted fun.

Food and Delicacies

Uttarayan is also a time for indulging in special delicacies. Undhiyu, a mixed vegetable dish traditionally cooked in earthen pots over an open fire, is a staple. It's a flavorful and nutritious dish made with seasonal winter vegetables. Til Laddu (sesame seed sweets) and Chikki (brittle made with peanuts or sesame seeds and jaggery) are also popular, providing warmth and energy during the winter season. These foods are shared among family and friends, further enhancing the festive spirit.

Sankraant (Madhya Pradesh)

Surya Arghya in Madhya Pradesh.

The festival is marked by specific rituals, including taking holy dips in rivers, especially the Narmada, considered sacred. This ritual is believed to cleanse sins and bring good fortune. Giving donations (daan) is also a crucial aspect, with people donating food, clothes, and other necessities to the needy.

Local Traditions and Rituals

A significant ritual is the offering of arghya (water offering) to the sun god (Surya) early in the morning. This is a way of expressing gratitude for the sun's life-giving energy. People also perform puja (worship) at home, offering prayers for prosperity and well-being. The act of giving daan is considered highly meritorious. Items like blankets, warm clothes, and food grains are often donated, especially to Brahmins and the less fortunate. In some regions, people also fly kites, although it's not as prevalent as in Gujarat.

Food and Festivities

Til Ladoo and Khichdi are the most prominent food items associated with Sankraant in Madhya Pradesh. Til Ladoo is not only a delicious treat but also symbolic, with sesame seeds representing immortality and jaggery symbolizing sweetness and prosperity. Khichdi is a simple yet nutritious dish that is often shared communally. Other sweets like Gajak (brittle made with sesame or peanuts and jaggery) and Revdi (sugar-coated sesame seeds) are also enjoyed.

Uttarayani (Uttarakhand)

Uttarayani in Uttarakhand is the local celebration of Makar Sankranti, marking the sun's northward journey (Uttarayan). It's most prominently celebrated in the Kumaon region and signifies the end of winter and the beginning of spring. The festival is marked by fairs, cultural performances, and traditional food.

The Uttarayani Mela

The Uttarayani Mela in Bageshwar has historical significance, dating back centuries. It was historically a major trading hub for the region, where merchants from different areas would gather to exchange goods. Today, the mela retains its commercial importance while also serving as a significant cultural event. The fair attracts thousands of people from nearby villages and towns, creating a lively and festive atmosphere. Stalls offer a variety of goods, from local handicrafts and woolen clothes to agricultural implements and household items. Food stalls offer traditional Kumaoni cuisine, providing a taste of local flavors. The mela is a vibrant display of Kumaoni culture and tradition.  

Cultural Performances and Traditions

The Jhora dance is a prominent part of Uttarayani celebrations. Performed in a circular formation, it involves rhythmic movements and synchronized steps. The accompanying songs often narrate local stories, myths, and legends, providing a glimpse into the region's rich cultural heritage. Other folk dances and musical performances are also common, adding to the festive atmosphere. In some areas, rituals related to the harvest and the changing season are performed, such as offering prayers for a bountiful harvest in the coming year. These cultural performances and rituals reinforce the community's connection to its traditions and heritage.

Bihu (Assam)

Bihu Celebrations.

Bihu is not a single festival but a set of three significant festivals celebrated in Assam, each marking a distinct phase in the agricultural cycle and Assamese life. These are: Rongali Bihu (Bohag Bihu), Kongali Bihu (Kati Bihu), and Magh Bihu (Bhogali Bihu).

  • Rongali Bihu (Bohag Bihu): Celebrated in April, marking the Assamese New Year and the spring festival.
  • Kongali Bihu (Kati Bihu): Celebrated in October-November, a somber festival associated with the completion of sowing and the scarcity of food.
  • Magh Bihu (Bhogali Bihu): Celebrated in January, coinciding with Makar Sankranti, marking the end of the harvest season. This is the Bihu most relevant to this article.

Magh Bihu (Bhogali Bihu)

Magh Bihu, also known as Bhogali Bihu, is directly connected to Makar Sankranti, sharing the theme of celebrating the harvest and the sun's northward journey. The term "Bhogali" signifies feasting and enjoyment, reflecting the abundance of food after the harvest

A central tradition of Magh Bihu is the construction of the "Meji," a bonfire made of bamboo, hay, and wood. On the eve of Magh Bihu, known as "Uruka," people gather around the Meji, offering prayers and throwing rice cakes (pitha) and other food items into the flames. The burning of the Meji symbolizes the burning away of the old year and the welcoming of a prosperous new one.

Another unique aspect of Magh Bihu is the construction of "Bhela Ghar," temporary huts made of bamboo, hay, and banana leaves. These huts are used for feasting and community gatherings during the Uruka night. People prepare and share traditional Assamese delicacies like Til Pitha (rice cakes filled with sesame seeds), Laru (coconut balls), Ghila Pitha (fried rice cakes), and Jolpan (a light meal of flattened rice, yogurt, and jaggery). The Uruka night is marked by community feasts, music, and merriment.
